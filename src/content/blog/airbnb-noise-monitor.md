The first time a neighbor knocked on my door at 11pm, I didn't have a noise monitor. My guests seemed fine when I checked in and I had no reason to think anything was wrong until my neighbor told me there were at least a dozen people in my two-bedroom unit. That was the moment a noise monitor became non-negotiable for me.

If you host on Airbnb, noise complaints are one of your biggest risks. They can cost you neighbors, permits, HOA standing, and five-star reviews. A noise monitor won't prevent every bad guest, but it gives you early warning before a situation turns into a full-blown incident.

This guide covers everything you need to know: what Airbnb's policy actually allows, which devices are worth considering, and how to use one without creating friction with your guests.

**Key Takeaways**
- Airbnb explicitly allows noise decibel monitors indoors as of its updated April 2024 policy
- Noise monitors measure sound levels only; they do not record audio or conversations
- You must disclose the monitor in your listing and show it in your listing photos
- The three devices most widely used by STR hosts are Minut, NoiseAware, and Alertify
- Setting the right alert threshold and response protocol is what makes a monitor actually useful

## What Airbnb's Policy Actually Says

A lot of hosts assume noise monitors exist in a gray area on Airbnb. They don't. As of April 30, 2024, Airbnb explicitly allows noise decibel monitors inside your rental as long as you:

1. Disclose the device in your listing description
2. Show it in your listing photos
3. Use a device that measures decibel levels only, with no audio recording capability

What Airbnb does not allow: any recording device in indoor spaces. That includes security cameras in hallways, bedrooms, bathrooms, living rooms, or any shared interior area. Hidden cameras have always been prohibited.

The distinction matters. A noise monitor reads decibel levels the same way a weather station reads temperature. It tells you "the property reached 85 dB at 11:42pm" but it has no idea what anyone said or who said it. That's the key difference between a noise monitor and a recording device.

Put the disclosure in your listing description and add a photo of the device in your listing photos. Something simple in your house rules works well: "This property uses a noise decibel monitor in common areas to help us respond to noise issues quickly. It does not record audio or video." Guests who take issue with that will self-select out before booking, which is actually ideal.

## The Noise Monitors Worth Considering

There are three devices that have become standard in the STR hosting community. Here's what you need to know about each.

**Minut**

Minut is the most widely used noise monitor among STR hosts. It measures decibels, temperature, humidity, and motion, and sends real-time alerts to your phone when noise crosses a threshold you set. It connects to Wi-Fi and integrates with most major property management platforms including Hospitable, Guesty, and OwnerRez. Pricing starts around $99 per device with an optional subscription for advanced features.

One standout feature: Minut sends a gentle automated message to guests when noise spikes, before alerting you. That first automated nudge resolves most situations without any host involvement.

**NoiseAware**

NoiseAware was one of the first privacy-safe noise monitors built specifically for vacation rentals. It measures indoor and outdoor noise separately, which is useful for properties with outdoor entertaining spaces. It's subscription-based with higher monthly costs than Minut, but hosts with larger portfolios or properties that have yards and patios tend to prefer it.

**Alertify**

Alertify is a newer option that combines noise monitoring with occupancy detection, using Wi-Fi device counting to estimate how many people are present. It's a good choice if overcrowding is a concern at your property, because noise alone doesn't always tell the whole story. You could have 20 quiet people or 4 loud ones, and Alertify helps you understand the difference.

For most single-property hosts, Minut is the most cost-effective starting point. If your property has an outdoor area where noise carries, NoiseAware's dual indoor/outdoor capability makes it worth the higher cost.

## Where to Place Your Noise Monitor

Placement matters. You want coverage of the spaces most likely to generate complaints while keeping the device out of areas where guests reasonably expect privacy.

Common areas are the right call: living rooms, kitchens, or open-plan main spaces. Avoid bedrooms and bathrooms entirely. Beyond guest comfort, monitoring sleeping or private spaces can violate local privacy laws depending on your city.

For my Long Beach properties, I put the monitor in the living room and kitchen area where guests congregate. That covers the primary noise source, and it's the part of the unit my neighbors can hear most clearly. The monitor has caught two situations early enough to resolve them before any complaint reached Airbnb.

If you have a multi-level property, one monitor per floor is a reasonable setup. You don't need one in every room to get good coverage.

## How to Set Alert Thresholds That Actually Work

The default thresholds that come with most devices are a starting point, not a final answer. You need to calibrate based on your property layout and your local quiet hours.

A typical approach that works well:
- **Warning alert**: 75 to 80 dB, sent first to guests automatically (normal conversation registers around 60 dB; 75 dB is roughly a vacuum cleaner)
- **Host alert**: 85+ dB sustained for more than 5 to 10 minutes, sent directly to you

Most devices let you set separate daytime and nighttime thresholds, which mirrors how most city noise ordinances are written. If your city has a 10pm quiet time, set your nighttime alert to trigger around 9:45pm so you have time to message guests before the ordinance kicks in.

Have a message template ready before the first alert fires. Something like: "Hi! I got an alert that noise levels are running a bit high. Just a quick heads-up that quiet hours in the area start at 10pm. Thanks so much for being considerate of the neighbors." Short, friendly, non-accusatory. Most guests respond well to that tone.

Your house manual is the right place to document this expectation in advance. If you haven't built one yet, take a look at the [complete guide to writing an Airbnb house manual](/blog/airbnb-house-manual) for a solid framework.

## How to Tell Guests About Your Noise Monitor

Disclosure is not just a policy requirement. It's a guest experience decision. Hosts who are upfront about noise monitors tend to have fewer conflicts than those who bury the disclosure in fine print.

Mention the monitor in three places: your listing description, your house rules section, and your digital welcome book. In the welcome book, include a photo of where the device is located and a one-sentence explanation of what it does and doesn't do.

That level of transparency has never led to a cancellation in my experience. Most guests appreciate knowing you take noise seriously, because it also signals that you maintain the property and care about the neighborhood. For some guests, it's actually reassuring.

If you use a digital welcome book for guests, include a short note about the monitor alongside your house rules. The [Complete Airbnb Guidebook template](https://shanadesigns.gumroad.com/l/airbnb-guidebook-template) has a dedicated section for house rules and property notes where this fits naturally, and it's easy to update the language for your specific device.

## A Note on Multi-Unit Properties

If you host both units at one property, as I do in Long Beach, noise monitoring takes on another dimension. You're managing noise between your two units as well as noise that carries to neighbors.

A few things that work well for multi-unit setups:
- Put a monitor in each unit separately; don't try to cover both with one device
- Set your thresholds conservatively, since you're essentially protecting two groups of guests from each other
- Be clear in your check-in instructions about shared walls and courtesy hours

Noise complaints between your own guests are one of the trickier situations in STR hosting because you're balancing two sets of expectations at once. A monitor gives you an objective reference point to work from instead of a he-said-she-said situation.

A noise monitor is a small investment that pays back quickly. One avoided security deposit dispute, one neighbor complaint that doesn't escalate to a city report, one party you shut down before it becomes an eviction situation. The cost is negligible compared to any of those outcomes.
